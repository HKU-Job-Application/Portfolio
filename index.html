Interactive Presentation: What does dx mean in an integral?
  • Inspired by findings by professors at Oregon State University, that many science students have no conceptual understanding of what they're integrating in multivariable calculus and how to properly interpret the differential (click here, here or here)
  • My presentation explains what a Riemann integral of a continuous function is and uses substitution of variables to explain why the differential is a measure, starting with one-variable calculus before going on to multi-variable calculus
  • It tries to be thorough without being technical or mathematically formal (because it is aimed at non-mathematics majors), and it includes lots of visualisations and graphs that are connected to each other on the same page (e.g. clicking on a small portion of a disc on one graph in R^2 automatically makes a small volume show up in red on another graph in R^3)[1]
  • https://hku-job-application.github.io/What-does-dx-mean-in-an-integral-/1.html
[1] My approach seems to accord with some remarks made in the paper:
Spencer-Tyree, Brielle, Bradley D. Bowen, and Modiu Olaguro. "The Impact of Computational Labs on Conceptual and Contextual Understanding in a Business Calculus Course." International Journal of Research in Undergraduate Mathematics Education 11, no. 3 (August 19, 2024): 514–37.
https://doi.org/10.1007/s40753-024-00255-1.

In particular, the Literature Review section opens with this paragraph about lack of conceptual understanding:
Students worldwide need help with conceptual understanding of calculus, often finishing the course with little to no conceptual knowledge intended by the curriculum (Epstein, 2013). A nationwide study in the United States found that in any introductory calculus class, about one-fourth of students either withdraw or earn a grade of D or F1 (Bressoud, 2015). This same study found that about two-thirds of calculus students in the United States self-reported difficulty in understanding derivatives and integrals. Only 40% of these students felt confident using derivatives and integrals in problem-solving scenarios (Bressoud, 2015), raising concerns about the extent of learning occurring in these courses (Breidenbach et al., 1992; Tallman et al., 2016; Thompson, 1994). Consequently, students remain marginally prepared for other mathematics courses due to the lack of understanding of the ideas foundational to calculus (Carlson, 1995; Selden et al., 2000; Thompson, 1994), resulting in a further lack of interest in mathematics in general (Bressoud et al., 2015; Seymour, 2006).
The paper also suggests that visualisation is super helpful for the students. A section titled "Theme 2: Visualisations" has several quotes from students appreciative of teaching methods by which they can see visually what is happening to the functions they need to use, without which they would struggle.
